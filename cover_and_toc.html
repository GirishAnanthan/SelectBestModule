Executive Insights
Financial Attractiveness
At a post-tax project IRR of 10.90% and equity IRR of 12.39%, the Project delivers a positive NPV of ₹26.89 Cr against a 9.07% blended WACC. A levelised cost of energy of ₹1.86/kWh sits comfortably below the contracted ₹3.50/kWh tariff, a margin that underwrites the return profile even after BESS integration costs.
Debt Servicing Capability
The 60:40 debt:equity structure at a 9.5% cost of debt produces a minimum DSCR of 1.49x and average DSCR of 1.66x across the loan tenor, both clear of the 1.20–1.25x threshold typically required by Indian project lenders. Minimum LLCR (1.70x) and PLCR (1.89x) confirm adequate coverage across the debt tenor and the full project life respectively.
Revenue Stability
Revenue is anchored by a 25-year bilateral PPA at a fixed ₹3.50/kWh tariff, generating approximately 103.54 MU of Year-1 output at a 24% CUF. The co-located 6 MWh BESS layer adds an incremental peak-saving revenue stream of ₹0.76 Cr/year without displacing the core PV cash flow, limiting exposure to any single revenue source.
Investment Highlights
Total CapEx of ₹213.49 Cr (₹4.27 Cr/MW) is financed on a conservative 40% equity base. BESS CapEx additionally benefits from a ₹1.08 Cr Viability Gap Funding grant that reduces the capital base before debt is sized — both factors improve the equity cash flow profile relative to a higher-leverage structure.
Major Risks
The primary watch-item is a thin equity IRR spread — 12.39% against a 12.00% cost-of-equity hurdle leaves limited headroom for CapEx overrun, tariff renegotiation, or generation shortfall. Additional risks include counterparty credit risk under the bilateral PPA and the absence of any REC or carbon-credit revenue overlay in the current model that could otherwise provide additional cushion.

The principal condition for continued monitoring is the equity return spread over the assumed hurdle rate, which should be revisited if CapEx, tariff, or generation assumptions change materially during financial close diligence.

Project Description
The Project is a 50 MW AC (62.5 MWp DC) ground-mount solar photovoltaic plant, co-located with a 6 MWh / 5 MW Battery Energy Storage System, sited on approximately 225 acres in Ramanathapuram district, Tamil Nadu — a coastal belt selected for its high solar irradiance and access to open agricultural land. The Project will supply power to the offtaker under a 25-year bilateral Power Purchase Agreement at a fixed tariff of ₹3.50/kWh, with the BESS layer adding incremental peak-saving revenue through daily charge-discharge cycling on top of the core PV cash flow.
Project Objectives
  • Deliver competitively priced solar power under a 25-year bilateral PPA at ₹3.50/kWh.
  • Integrate a 6 MWh BESS layer to capture peak-saving revenue and improve grid dispatchability.
  • Achieve a 24% CUF by leveraging Tamil Nadu's high-irradiance coastal resource.
  • Contribute to Tamil Nadu's renewable capacity addition targets under the state RE policy framework.

Professional Analysis
Insurance is the largest single OPEX line at 43.5% of Year-1 cost (₹2.02 Cr), exceeding core O&M (₹1.79 Cr combined PV+BESS). This stems from a 0.99% annual premium rate applied to the full insurable asset value — a rate on the higher end of typical Machinery Breakdown + Business Interruption cover for Indian utility-scale solar, and should be validated against an actual insurer quote rather than the current estimate-based assumption.
At a 5.72% p.a. escalation rate, annual OPEX grows 3.2× over the project life, from ₹4.65 Cr in Year 1 to ₹14.97 Cr in Year 25. Cumulative 25-year OPEX of ₹218.43 Cr exceeds total project CAPEX (₹213.49 Cr) — a structurally normal outcome for a 25-year operating asset, but one that underscores how sensitive long-run project economics are to the escalation assumption.
Administration (₹0.02 Cr) is immaterial and, unlike every other OPEX line, does not escalate over the project life — this is a modeling simplification worth flagging, as real administrative costs typically inflate with wage growth over a 25-year horizon.

Professional Commentary
Revenue is almost entirely PPA-driven (97.9% of Year 1), with Battery Revenue contributing a modest incremental 2.1%. This is a low-diversification revenue structure by design — a single fixed-price 25-year bilateral contract — which maximizes revenue predictability but means the Project carries no upside from REC, carbon credit, or merchant market participation, all of which remain available toggles in the model if the commercial structure changes.
Because the ₹3.50/kWh tariff has zero escalation, total revenue is a pure function of declining generation: from ₹37.00 Cr in Year 1 to ₹29.75 Cr in Year 25, a 19.6% decline that tracks the modeled degradation curve exactly (100% → 80% of Year-1 output). This is the mirror image of the OPEX trend (Section 05), which grows 3.2× over the same period — the combination compresses operating margin materially in the back half of the project life and is a key driver of the DSCR profile discussed in the Debt Analysis section.
Battery Revenue is flat at ₹0.76 Cr/year for all 25 years — the model does not currently apply the BESS spread-decline mechanism (storage cannibalization) used for EU projects, so this should be treated as a conservative-to-optimistic assumption depending on how quickly Tamil Nadu's ToD tariff spread narrows as more storage capacity comes online regionally.

Professional Recommendations
The Project's zero merchant exposure is its single strongest market-risk characteristic: with 100% of output contracted under a fixed 25-year PPA, none of curtailment, capture-rate compression, negative pricing, or spot-price volatility can affect modeled revenue. This should be preserved as a core credit strength through financial close — any renegotiation toward partial merchant sale would introduce market risk not currently priced into the DSCR or IRR figures elsewhere in this report.
The distinction between Transmission Loss (IPP-borne, 1.5%, reduces revenue by ₹0.55 Cr/year) and Grid/Distribution Loss (DISCOM-borne, 3.4%, reference only) should be confirmed against the actual grid connection agreement — if the interconnection point or loss allocation differs from this assumption, the revenue impact could be materially larger than currently modeled.
Ahead of financial close, we recommend confirming plant availability guarantees (O&M contractor SLA) and grid congestion risk at the specific interconnection substation directly with the DISCOM/transmission utility — neither is currently captured in this financial model and both are common real-world drivers of shortfall versus a P50 generation estimate.
